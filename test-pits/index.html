Exploratory Test Pits in Drummondville: Subsurface Verification for Foundation Design

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

LEARN MORE

The soil profile shifts noticeably between the older Saint-Joseph district and the newer developments near the Saint-François River. In the older core of Drummondville, you might encounter well-compacted granular deposits overlying glacial till, ideal for conventional footings. Move east toward the river terraces, however, and the picture changes completely—interbedded silts and sands with organic lenses are common, making a visual inspection of the subsurface essential before any structural commitment. An exploratory test pit in Drummondville provides that direct window into the ground, allowing the geotechnical team to log stratigraphy, identify fill boundaries, and take undisturbed samples right where the future foundation will sit. The method aligns with ASTM D2488 for visual-manual classification and is particularly useful in Drummondville's mixed glacial and alluvial terrain, where the liquefaction susceptibility of loose saturated sands near the water table demands careful evaluation during the pit inspection.

A single exploratory pit often reveals more about Drummondville's Champlain Sea stratigraphy than a full set of SPT boreholes—seeing is understanding.

Methodology and scope

A three-storey mixed-use building on Saint-Pierre Boulevard presented an interesting challenge last season. The borehole logs from a previous campaign suggested uniform dense sand, but an exploratory test pit in Drummondville excavated to 3.2 meters revealed a discontinuous layer of soft clayey silt at 2.1 meters—something the drilled samples had completely missed. That single observation changed the foundation recommendation from isolated footings to a stiffer mat system, avoiding differential settlement issues down the line. The strength of the exploratory test pit lies in exposing a continuous vertical face where the technician can measure layer thicknesses directly, photograph the in-situ structure, and collect block samples for laboratory testing. In Drummondville, where the post-glacial Champlain Sea deposits create complex interlayering of marine clays and deltaic sands, this visual continuity is invaluable. The pit log becomes a permanent reference for the structural engineer, showing not just soil types but also groundwater seepage patterns, oxidation stains indicating seasonal water table fluctuation, and root penetration depth from nearby mature trees. These observations feed directly into bearing capacity calculations per NBCC 2015 and CSA A23.3 requirements for concrete exposed to sulfate-rich soils.

Local considerations

A common mistake on Drummondville sites is assuming that competent-looking surface gravel extends to foundation depth without verification. More than once, crews have excavated for footing forms only to discover buried organic silt or old backfill from a demolished structure that was never documented on any record drawing. An exploratory test pit in Drummondville catches these surprises before the concrete truck arrives. The visual inspection reveals exactly what lies beneath—whether it's construction debris from the 1950s expansion of the city center, uncompacted fill placed during residential subdivision development in the 1970s, or natural soft clay lenses that would compress differentially under load. Skipping this step can lead to costly redesigns mid-construction, or worse, foundation performance issues that manifest years later as cracking in brick veneer and drywall. The NBCC 2015 requires that foundation design be based on adequate subsurface investigation; an exploratory test pit provides that adequacy in a cost-effective format that complements deeper borings.

What can a test pit reveal that a borehole might miss?

A test pit exposes a continuous vertical face, which reveals thin layers, lenses, and inclusions that can be missed between SPT spoon intervals in a borehole. In Drummondville's Champlain Sea deposits, thin seams of silt within clay or pockets of organic material are common and can control drainage and settlement behavior. The pit also shows the true structure of fill materials—brick fragments, wood, or debris layers—that a split-spoon sample might not represent accurately.

Is a test pit enough for foundation design, or do I also need boreholes?

It depends on the depth of influence of your foundation. For shallow footings within 2 to 3 meters of grade, a well-executed test pit program often provides sufficient information for bearing capacity and settlement analysis. For deeper load transfer—piles, caissons, or deep basements—boreholes with SPT or CPT data are necessary to characterize deeper strata. Many Drummondville projects combine one or two test pits with a few boreholes to cover both the near-surface variability and the deeper bearing layers.

How long does it take to excavate and log a test pit, and what about restoration?

A single test pit in Drummondville is typically excavated and logged within one site visit, usually 3 to 5 hours including mobilization, excavation, face cleaning, photography, sampling, and backfill. The pit is backfilled with the excavated material in lifts with compaction, and surface restoration—sod replacement, gravel regrading, or asphalt patch—is completed the same day. For sites with sensitive landscaping, a smaller excavator or hand-digging for the upper portion can be arranged to minimize disturbance.
