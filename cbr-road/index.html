Methodology and scope

The most common mistake we see contractors make in the Centre-du-Québec area is trusting the dry-season CBR. A silty clay subgrade that looks rock-hard in August can turn to jelly by mid-October. That’s why our laboratory procedure follows the standard 96-hour soaking period under a surcharge weight that simulates the overlying pavement structure. We compact the material at its optimum moisture content from a modified Proctor, then submerge it to measure the strength loss. The result is a design CBR that reflects the worst-case scenario, not the best. Pairing this with a grain-size analysis helps confirm whether the fine fraction is high enough to justify stabilization with lime or cement before paving.

Local considerations

Comparing the Saint-Nicéphore area with the older residential grid of Drummondville-Sud tells you everything about local soil variability. Saint-Nicéphore sits on sandier deposits left by glacial outwash, often delivering CBR values above 15% after compaction. Drummondville-Sud, closer to the river, deals with soft, compressible silts where CBR can drop below 3% after soaking. A designer who assumes one profile for the whole city is asking for differential settlement and premature fatigue cracking. The laboratory CBR test isolates this risk before truck traffic hits the asphalt. When results come back marginal, we often recommend a deeper subcut or chemical stabilization to bring the formation layer up to the MTQ minimum structural coefficient required for the design traffic loading.

How long does the laboratory CBR test take from sample submission to report?

The soaking period alone requires 4 days. With sample preparation, compaction, and reporting, plan on 6 to 7 working days. Expedited scheduling is available for projects facing tight construction windows.

Can we test granular base course materials with the lab CBR method?

Yes. While CBR is most critical for fine-grained subgrade, coarse base course aggregates can also be tested. The standard mold works well for material passing the 19 mm sieve. For larger aggregates, we follow the modified procedure using a larger mold or scalping to the appropriate size.

What CBR value does the MTQ require for residential streets in the region?

The MTQ typically expects a minimum soaked CBR of 8% on the prepared subgrade for local residential streets. If natural ground falls below that, the specification allows for a minimum 300 mm of additional granular subbase or lime stabilization to achieve the required structural number.

Do you pick up samples from job sites around Drummondville?

We coordinate sample collection with the drilling crew performing the site investigation. Bulk disturbed samples are sealed in plastic bags on site and transported to the lab within 48 hours to preserve natural moisture conditions before compaction.
