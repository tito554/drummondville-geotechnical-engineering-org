Field Permeability Testing Lefranc & Lugeon – Drummondville

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

LEARN MORE

Drummondville sits on the Saint-François River terraces, where historical brickworks and textile mills shaped early foundation engineering. The subsurface here alternates between dense glacial till, lacustrine silts, and coarse fluvial gravels. Permeability can shift two orders of magnitude in less than three meters. A standard percolation test misses these transitions entirely. We run the Lefranc method in soil boreholes and the Lugeon test in fractured rock sections of the Appalachian foothills, following ASTM D4630 and ASTM D4631. The data feeds directly into dewatering plans and cutoff wall designs. Before committing to a deep excavation, a CPT test can map the stratigraphic boundaries, and our test pits verify the contact zones between the till and the underlying St. Lawrence Lowlands sedimentary sequences.

One Lugeon test in fractured shale tells you more about groundwater flow than fifty lab permeameter runs.

Methodology and scope

The most common mistake we see in Drummondville: assuming a single permeability value from a grain-size correlation chart. The region's lodgement till contains silt lenses and fractured shale clasts that create preferential flow paths no lab test can replicate. In-situ Lefranc testing isolates specific depth intervals, measuring hydraulic conductivity under true field gradients. Lugeon testing in the Nicolet Formation shale quantifies fracture aperture and connectivity, critical for grouting decisions. We use pneumatic packers, constant-head or falling-head configurations, and calibrated flowmeters reading to ±2% accuracy. All test intervals are logged against recovered core and downhole geophysics. For projects near the Saint-François River, slope stability analysis often requires this permeability data to model pore pressure response during rapid drawdown scenarios.

Local considerations

The Lugeon setup for Drummondville's bedrock includes a high-pressure pump with a 10-bar capacity, a double-packer assembly lowered on AW rods, and a digital pressure transducer with data-logging at one-second intervals. In the till, the Lefranc test uses a slotted PVC screen, a constant-head reservoir, and a submersible pump to maintain steady flow. Clogging is the enemy. Fine silt from the Champlain Sea deposits migrates into the screen if the filter pack is poorly graded. We pre-flush each test section with clean water and monitor turbidity before recording measurements. A failed test in glacial-lacustrine sequences often means re-drilling the interval. The rig must stay on standby.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between Lefranc and Lugeon testing?

Lefranc tests measure hydraulic conductivity in granular soils and soft rock using a slotted casing section. Lugeon tests apply pressurized water through a packer-isolated interval in competent rock, quantifying fracture flow in Lugeon units. We select the method based on the recovered core log.

How long does a Lefranc test take in Drummondville's glacial till?

A single test interval typically requires 45 to 90 minutes, including pre-flushing, saturation stabilization, and data acquisition over three flow stages. In low-permeability silty till, the falling-head phase may extend to two hours to capture the full drainage curve.

Can Lugeon values be directly converted to hydraulic conductivity?

Yes, using the Moye equation or Hvorslev's formulation, we convert Lugeon units (liters/minute/meter at 10 bar) to equivalent hydraulic conductivity in m/s, accounting for fracture flow geometry and borehole diameter.
