Shallow Foundation Design in Drummondville: Bearing Capacity and Settlement Analysis

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

LEARN MORE

We see it repeatedly on Drummondville sites: a contractor pours strip footings in late autumn, backfills against the stem wall, and by March the foundation has heaved half an inch. The culprit is rarely the concrete mix. It is usually an oversight in the shallow foundation design stage, where the frost depth was assumed rather than verified against the actual groundwater condition at that specific lot. Drummondville sits at roughly 45.88 degrees north, and the NBCC prescribes a frost penetration depth that can reach 1.5 metres in exposed silts. A proper test pits investigation before finalizing the footing elevation would have caught the perched water table that accelerates frost lens growth. We design footings and mat foundations around these local variables, not against them. The Saint-François River alluvium varies from clean gravels on the higher terraces to thick compressible clay in the lowlands, and each requires a different bearing capacity verification and settlement control strategy under CSA A23.3 and NBCC Part 4. For larger commercial footings near the river, we often pair the shallow foundation analysis with a CPT test to profile the soft clay layers that control long-term consolidation settlement.

A shallow foundation on Champlain Sea clay needs a settlement estimate before the footing dimensions are locked. Skempton's bearing capacity formula alone is not enough.

Process and scope

The typical soil profile in the Saint-Charles sector of Drummondville consists of 0.5 to 1.2 metres of silty sand overlying a grey Champlain Sea clay that extends to depths exceeding 20 metres. The undrained shear strength in that clay unit can be as low as 30 kPa at 3 metres depth, which puts the allowable bearing pressure for a conventional strip footing right at the edge of the NBCC presumptive values. We do not rely on presumptive tables for these conditions. Our shallow foundation design workflow starts with a field identification program per ASTM D2488, then moves to laboratory strength testing on undisturbed Shelby tube samples. We calculate the short-term bearing capacity using Skempton's method for phi-zero conditions, then check the long-term drained case with effective stress parameters from consolidated-undrained triaxial tests. The settlement analysis runs both immediate elastic and consolidation phases, which is critical in Drummondville where the clay preconsolidation pressure often sits just 20-40 kPa above the current overburden stress. Any added footing load pushes the soil past its historical maximum stress, triggering primary consolidation settlements that can continue for 18 to 24 months after construction.

Local considerations

Drummondville's urban expansion followed the Saint-François River terraces, then pushed south into the till-covered uplands and east into the flat clay plains. That development pattern left a legacy of mixed foundation performance. Older homes in the Saint-Joseph district, built on shallow footings without reinforcement, show classic differential settlement cracking because the footings were placed at inconsistent depths above the frost line on clay that crept slowly under load. The real risk today is repeating that mistake on infill lots where the adjacent structures have already consolidated the clay. We measure the preconsolidation pressure on every project. If the planned footing stress plus the existing overburden exceeds that threshold, we either deepen the bearing level, widen the footing to reduce contact pressure, or switch to a rigid mat foundation that bridges soft spots. Another risk is frost jacking on the north side of buildings where solar exposure is minimal. We specify vertical insulation at the footing edge and horizontal wing insulation extending 1.2 metres outward in those cases, following the thermal modelling approach in the National Energy Code for Buildings.

Common questions

What soil conditions in Drummondville require a mat foundation instead of strip footings?

When the undrained shear strength of the clay drops below 30 kPa or the total settlement estimate for strip footings exceeds 25 mm, we recommend switching to a mat foundation. The mat distributes the building load over a larger area, reducing the contact pressure and bridging localized soft spots. We have used this approach on several commercial projects near the Autoroute 20 corridor where the clay thickness exceeded 15 metres and the water table was within 1 metre of grade.

How deep do footings need to be in Drummondville to avoid frost heave?

The NBCC minimum frost depth for the region is 1.5 metres below finished grade. However, that number assumes a worst-case freezing index and saturated fine-grained soil. If the site investigation shows the groundwater table is deeper than 2.5 metres and the upper soil is clean sand, we can sometimes justify a shallower depth with thermal modelling. We never make that call without a borehole log and a groundwater measurement taken in late winter when the water table is highest.

Do you check both short-term and long-term stability for footings on clay?

Yes. For the short-term, undrained condition, we use the undrained shear strength from field vane or triaxial tests with a bearing capacity factor from Skempton. For the long-term, drained condition, we use effective stress friction angles from consolidated-undrained triaxial tests with pore pressure measurement. The long-term case often governs in Drummondville clays because the effective friction angle can be as low as 22 degrees, and the drained bearing capacity is lower than the undrained value when the water table is near the footing level.
