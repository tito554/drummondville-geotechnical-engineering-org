Grouting Design for Complex Ground Conditions in Drummondville

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

LEARN MORE

The Saint-François River carved a complicated subsurface across Drummondville long before the first foundation was poured. Glacial till overlies fractured shale and sandstone, but the transition is rarely clean. You find lenses of loose alluvium, buried organic silts near the old river channels, and weathered bedrock that crumbles under the drill bit. Sealing or strengthening these zones demands a grouting design that adapts to what the borehole actually reveals, not what the regional map assumes. We combine field data from in-situ permeability testing with grout rheology models to specify injection pressures, stage lengths, and mix formulations that match the real permeability contrast between till and bedrock. The approach avoids both surface leaks and incomplete penetration, two problems we see repeatedly in Drummondville's post-glacial deposits.

Groutability in Drummondville's glacial till is rarely uniform: the design must switch from permeation to compaction logic within the same borehole array.

Methodology and scope

CSA A23.3 provides the concrete and grout material framework, but the injection methodology for ground improvement falls under the broader intent of the NBCC and geotechnical practice guides from the Canadian Foundation Engineering Manual. In Drummondville, the design must account for a specific challenge: the bedrock surface is often steeply undulating, with troughs filled by compressible silts that cannot be removed for every structure. Permeation grouting with microfine cements works well in the silty sand lenses above the till, while compaction grouting becomes necessary where the overburden is too loose to support shallow footings. Where we encounter fractured shale with open joints, the design shifts to high-mobility grouts sequenced in primary-secondary holes. A thorough grain-size analysis of the soil fractions dictates the groutability ratio, which is the single most important parameter to avoid wasting material in ungroutable formations. We cross-check this with atterberg-limits to understand how the silt and clay fractions will react to pressure injection without hydrofracturing the overburden.

Local considerations

Drummondville sits at roughly 365 m elevation within a moderate seismic zone (NBCC 2020 spectral acceleration Sa(0.2) around 0.3–0.4 g). While not the highest seismic hazard in Québec, the combination of soft alluvial pockets and a shallow, irregular bedrock profile creates amplification patterns that concentrate strain at the soil-rock interface. Uncontrolled seepage through fractured bedrock beneath a compacted fill can erode the fine fraction of the till, leading to settlement that appears years after construction. The bigger operational risk is over-injection: applying high pressure in unconfined zones near the riverbank can daylight grout into the Saint-François or its tributary streams, triggering environmental compliance violations. Our design method includes a step-rate injection test for every distinct stratum, establishing the fracture pressure empirically rather than assuming a textbook value. This protects both the ground and the contractor from the two costliest outcomes: incomplete treatment and environmental release.

Frequently asked questions

What type of grout works best in Drummondville’s glacial till?

It depends on the fines content. For silty sand lenses with less than 12% passing the 75 µm sieve, microfine cement grouts with a w/c ratio between 1.5 and 2.0 provide good penetrability. Where silt content exceeds 15–20%, permeation is no longer reliable—the design switches to compaction grouting with a stiff, low-slump mix or to jet grouting if a continuous treated zone is required. We always run a grain-size curve and Atterberg limits before finalizing the grout type.

How do you verify that the grouting has worked?

Verification is built into the design from the start. We specify pre-treatment in-situ permeability tests (falling head or constant head) and repeat them post-treatment in check holes. For compaction grouting, we compare SPT N-values or CPT tip resistance before and after treatment. Rock fissure grouting programs include Lugeon testing in verification holes to confirm the residual permeability meets the project target, typically below 1–5 Lugeon units for cutoff applications.

Do you design grouting for environmental cutoff walls near the Saint-François River?

Yes. Seepage cutoff walls using low-permeability grout curtains are common for excavations and foundations near the river. The design must account for groundwater flow velocity to avoid grout washout before setting, and we specify rapid-set admixtures when needed. Environmental monitoring points are included in the QA/QC plan to confirm no grout migration into surface water.

How long does it take to prepare a grouting design?

A complete design package—from receiving the site investigation data to issuing the signed method statement—typically takes 7 to 14 business days. Complex sites with multiple treatment zones or difficult access may require additional time for iterative analysis. We can fast-track the initial parameter specification if the contractor needs to mobilize equipment while the full report is finalized.
