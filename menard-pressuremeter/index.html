Ménard Pressuremeter Test (PMT) in Drummondville – Reliable Soil Stiffness & Strength Data

Site investigations you can build on.

LEARN MORE

The difference between building on the dense glacial till near the Saint-François River and the soft marine clay of the Saint-Nicéphore sector is night and day. One gives you a stiff bearing layer, the other behaves more like a viscous fluid under load. That’s why in Drummondville we rely heavily on the Ménard pressuremeter test (PMT) to quantify exactly how each soil horizon will react to a foundation load. Unlike drilling and sampling alone, the PMT gives us a direct measurement of the soil’s deformation modulus (EM) and limit pressure (pl) right in the borehole. For a project on the clay plain near the Centre Marcel-Dionne, we can run a PMT profile every 1.5 m and produce a complete stiffness curve down to 15 m. It’s the only way to get reliable settlement predictions in a region where the natural water content often exceeds 80%.

A single PMT profile in Drummondville’s marine clay can reduce foundation cost by 15% by eliminating unnecessary overdesign of the footing width.

Process overview

The probe itself is a three-cell cylindrical bladder, 44 mm in diameter when deflated, that we lower into a carefully prepared borehole. Once at the test depth, we inflate it stepwise with compressed nitrogen while a control unit records the volume injected versus the pressure applied. A typical test takes about 12 minutes per level, and we can complete six to eight tests in a single 12 m borehole in one day. The equipment fits in a standard pickup truck, so we mobilize quickly across Drummondville. We always pair the PMT with a granulometry test to understand the soil’s gradation and with Atterberg limits to classify the clay’s plasticity, because the pressuremeter modulus alone doesn’t tell you why the soil behaves that way. Calibration of the probe is done inside a thick-walled steel cylinder at the start of each project, following ASTM. We also run a permeability test in the field on the same borehole when the project involves a basement excavation below the water table.

Local context

Drummondville grew fast during the 1950s and 1960s, and a lot of that expansion happened on the Leda clay that underlies the eastern half of the city. That clay has a sensitivity of 20 to 40, meaning when disturbed it loses most of its strength. We’ve seen cases where a shallow footing designed using standard penetration test (SPT) N-values alone ended up settling 8 cm more than the structural engineer allowed. The PMT catches that risk early because the creep pressure derived from the test tells you exactly at what stress level the soil starts to yield. For any project near the old railway corridor or along Boul. Saint-Joseph, we consider the PMT mandatory if the building has more than two stories.

Q&A

How does the PMT differ from an SPT in Drummondville's clay?

The SPT gives you a blow count that correlates to relative density, but in soft clay the N-value is often zero and tells you nothing about stiffness. The PMT directly measures the deformation modulus EM and the limit pressure pl, which are the parameters you actually need for settlement and bearing capacity calculations in Drummondville’s sensitive marine clay.

What is the typical cost range for a Ménard pressuremeter test in Drummondville?

A standard PMT profile with six tests in one borehole, including mobilization within the city limits, a written report with modulus and limit pressure values, and a bearing capacity recommendation, falls between CA$1.290 and CA$1.620. The exact price depends on the number of test levels and the total borehole depth.

Can you run the PMT in winter on frozen ground?

Yes, we can. The borehole is drilled using a truck-mounted rig with a carbide-tipped bit that cuts through the frozen crust. The pressuremeter probe operates normally below freezing because the control unit uses nitrogen gas. We have completed PMT campaigns in Drummondville during January and February with no loss of data quality.
