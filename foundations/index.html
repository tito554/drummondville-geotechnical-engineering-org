Foundations in Drummondville

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

LEARN MORE

In Drummondville, foundation engineering must address the Champlain Sea clay deposits and variable glacial till that define the region’s subsurface. Our category spans geotechnical investigations, bearing capacity assessments, and deep foundation solutions aligned with the National Building Code of Canada and CSA standards. For sites with compressible soils, pile foundation design transfers loads to competent strata, while our driven pile analysis verifies performance under local seismic and frost conditions.

Residential, commercial, and industrial projects on low-strength silts or near the Saint-François River often require advanced solutions. We support bridge abutments, warehouse slabs, and mid-rise structures with deep foundation testing to confirm integrity and settlement control. Whether reinforcing existing footings or designing new systems, our approach ensures compliance with Quebec’s geotechnical requirements and long-term stability in the region’s sensitive soils.

What is the difference between active and passive anchors?

Active anchors are post-tensioned after grouting to apply a predetermined force against the retaining structure; they use an unbonded free length to stretch the tendon. Passive anchors develop resistance only when the ground moves and loads them, working through full-length bond without prestress. Active systems suit stiff clays where movement must be minimized; passive bars are often better in mixed soils where bond reliability varies.

How long do permanent anchors last in Champlain Sea clay?

Permanent anchors in Drummondville’s Champlain clay require double-corrosion encapsulation (Class II protection) per PTI recommendations. With proper grouting and a minimum 75 mm cover over the encapsulation, the design life exceeds 50 years. The main durability risk is sulfate attack on the grout, addressed by using sulfate-resistant cement where groundwater sulfate concentrations exceed 150 ppm.
