Commercial Slope Stability Analysis in Drummondville

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

Contractors working the terraces above the Saint-François River know the routine: you strip the topsoil, hit that compact grey-brown till, and wonder how the 3:1 cut will hold through a spring thaw. Drummondville sits on a mix of glacial deposits and post-Champlain Sea silts that behave well enough in summer but turn unpredictable when saturation hits. We run slope stability analysis here almost weekly during excavation season, and the one thing that saves a project timeline is having a factor of safety number you can show the municipality. Our lab holds ISO 17025 accreditation and every report follows the National Building Code of Canada, with shear strength parameters pulled directly from local borehole data. When the till is thinner than expected, we recommend pairing the analysis with a CPT investigation to confirm refusal depth before finalizing the cut geometry.

A factor of safety of 1.0 means the slope is thinking about moving. We design for 1.5 long-term because Drummondville clay does not give second chances.

Methodology and scope

The NBCC 2015 Part 4 requires explicit stability verification for any permanent slope exceeding 2 m in height, and Drummondville's zoning enforcement has tightened since the 2019 landslide scares in the Saint-Maurice valley. Our approach here relies on limit equilibrium methods—Bishop simplified for circular failures, Spencer for non-circular—using soil parameters calibrated against ASTM D4767 triaxial data from the actual formation, not textbook values. The Saint-François terraces often present a layered profile: desiccated crust over soft silty clay over dense till, and that middle layer controls the failure mechanism. We model both drained and undrained conditions because the water table in Centre-du-Québec can rise two metres between August and April. For steep cuts near residential foundations, combining the analysis with retaining wall design often yields a more economical solution than benching alone, especially where right-of-way is tight.

Local considerations

Drummondville's industrial expansion since the 1960s pushed development onto marginal land along the Saint-François floodplain, where old meander scars filled with compressible organic silt lurk under a thin crust of fill. We have pulled bore logs from a 2018 warehouse project near Saint-Majorique where the clay softened to N-values of 3 at just four metres depth—a classic setup for a deep-seated rotational failure if the slope toe gets undercut during utility trenching. The worst-case scenario is a wet October after a dry summer: cracks open in the crust, rainwater pipes straight down to the slickensided clay surface, and a block of soil the size of a parking lot starts creeping toward the river. A proper slope stability analysis catches this before the backhoe arrives, quantifying the risk in terms the project owner's insurer will accept. The cost of stabilizing a failing slope mid-construction runs three to five times higher than getting the analysis done upfront.

What triggers a mandatory slope stability review under the Drummondville building code?

Any permanent excavation or fill slope steeper than 2:1 (horizontal:vertical) and taller than 2 metres requires an engineered stability analysis per NBCC Part 4. The Drummondville building department also requests it for developments within 50 metres of the Saint-François River bank, regardless of slope height, due to erosion concerns.

How long does the analysis take from field investigation to final report?

Allow three to four weeks for a typical Drummondville project. Drilling and sampling takes two to three days on site, our lab needs ten to twelve business days for triaxial consolidation and shear stages, and the modeling and report drafting runs another week. Rush turnaround is available for an additional fee during the spring construction window.

Can you analyze an existing slope that is already showing tension cracks?

Yes, and we treat those as urgent. We drill behind the crack zone, sample the failure plane material, run residual shear strength tests (ASTM D3080 remolded), and back-calculate the current factor of safety. The report identifies whether drainage, unloading the crest, or installing a shear key is the most cost-effective stabilization method.
